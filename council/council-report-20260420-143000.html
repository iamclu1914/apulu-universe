Advisor Responses

The Contrarian — this will fail; here's the likeliest failure mode

This fails the same way your last two mix systems are failing — you keep building scaffolding around a source-quality problem that no signal chain can fix.

You already have vawn-mix-engine with documented bugs actively being patched (param map drift, Mix Assistant crushing bass on Suno bass stems, LUFS gain staging, Sculptor scooping fundamentals). You ALSO have Ai Mix Engineer/backend — a FastAPI service with its own agent orchestrator. Neither has hit "near professional." Building a third tool is how you end up with three half-broken systems and no finished mixes.

The bottleneck is not software architecture. It's decisions that require ears in the loop: which Suno generation is worth mixing vs. regenerating, when to re-separate with UVR5 vs. use Suno's native stems, when to replace AI drums with Splice samples vs. treat them, whether the 16kHz fizz on THIS particular track warrants a low-pass or a dynamic EQ. No program automates taste.

Second failure: iZotope's AI Assistants were trained on studio recordings, not AI-generated audio. You're stacking Suno's AI → iZotope's AI → your custom logic. That chain is unpredictable by design. More automation layers on top makes it worse.

Third: your "no mastering" stance means the mix carries 100% of the perceptual work. That's backwards from how professional workflows run — mixing gets you 90%, mastering closes the last 10%. If your mastering service is LANDR-style pass-through, it's doing nothing and you're asking the mix to carry a burden it was never designed to carry.

The First Principles Thinker — the question as posed is probably wrong

The question as posed is wrong. "Build a program to mix Suno stems" presumes the answer is code. Strip that assumption out.

What's actually being solved? You want Suno tracks to sound near-professional. That problem decomposes into three independent layers:

  • Layer 1 — Source quality at ingest. Suno's native stem separation leaves bleed. No mix chain fixes bad source. Do Suno's native stems pass, or do they need UVR5 re-separation first?
  • Layer 2 — AI-specific artifact profile. 16kHz shimmer, metallic resonances, over-compressed dynamics. This is a restoration problem, not a mixing problem. It happens BEFORE Nectar or Neutron touch the audio.
  • Layer 3 — Musical mixing. Balance, EQ, compression, spatial placement. Vawn-mix-engine already does this.

Your current engine collapses Layers 2 and 3 into one pass. Nectar and Neutron don't know the stem has AI shimmer — they mix it as if it's a studio recording. That's why the output feels "off" despite correct mixing.

You don't need a new program. You need a Stage 0 — restoration — inserted before Stage 1 in vawn-mix-engine. The right question: "How do I add an AI-restoration pre-stage that uses RX 11 and optionally UVR5 re-separation, and writes decision metadata per stem?" That's a one-week extension, not a new architecture.

The Expansionist — the upside is bigger than you're imagining

You're thinking about this at 10% of its real size.

A tool that mixes your own Suno tracks? Fine. A tool that mixes every indie artist's Suno tracks? That's a product. Every bedroom producer using Suno has the same problem — AI shimmer, muddy low-end, crushed dynamics, separation bleed. Most don't own iZotope. Most don't use REAPER. Most will never figure out a five-stage processing chain. You already have one.

Package it. "SunoFix for REAPER" — a REAPER extension that installs your templates, runs your restoration chain, outputs mix-ready stems. $49 one-time, $9/month for updates. Suno has millions of users.

Bigger vision: Apulu Records isn't just a label, it's the reference implementation for how AI music gets professionally produced. Paperclip, vawn-mix-engine, the Ai Mix Engineer backend, research pipelines, marketing dispatcher — nobody else has this stack. The category "AI music production infrastructure" doesn't exist yet. You're 18 months ahead of where major labels will be forced to go.

10x version: skip REAPER entirely. Standalone web app. Suno stems in → UVR5 re-sep in cloud → iZotope-inspired processing → mix-ready stems out. Every Suno track on Earth passes through your pipe. That's not a mix tool. That's infrastructure.

The Outsider — zero context, everything's confusing

I don't know what Suno is. I don't know what REAPER is. I've never heard of iZotope. So when you say you want a program for "near-professional mixing" — professional compared to what?

A radio pop song? A Kendrick Lamar record? A SoundCloud indie release? An Ariana Grande single? These sound nothing like each other. "Professional" isn't a single target. Which specific song, played back to back against your Suno track, makes you say "this is the gap I want to close"?

Second thing I'm confused about: you said mastering is handled by another service. In every audio production workflow I've heard of, mixing and mastering interact. So if your mix doesn't sound professional — how do you know that's a mix problem and not a mastering problem? Have you taken the exact same stems, had a human engineer mix them, run them through your mastering service, and compared? If a human mix through the same mastering service also fails, the mastering is the weak link.

Simple question nobody's asked: what specifically sounds wrong to YOU right now? "Near professional" is vague. "My 808s sound like cardboard compared to Metro Boomin" is specific. "My vocals sound thin compared to J. Cole" is specific. The answer depends on which.
